Van Gogh's Starlit Faith: The Subtle Notes of a Divine Creator


Dutch Post-Impressionist artist Vincent van Gogh once said to his brother Theo, "It does me well to undertake what's tough. That doesn't stop me from feeling a strong desire for religion, if you will, so I walk outside at night to paint the sky." This powerful admission says a lot about van Gogh's connection with faith, the perceived hardship of his artistic journey, and how these things interact.

Even while the stars are not depicted in the majority of van Gogh's 900 paintings, those that do have become his most celebrated works of art. The most well-known of these is "The Starry Night," a work of art that graces the Museum of Modern Art in New York and is without a doubt profound and beautiful. "Starry Night Over the Rhône," painted in southern France, is a less well-known but no less important work. It features the Big Dipper positioned above the city lights of Arles, their reflection dancing in the river, and two figures standing in the foreground.

Van Gogh's sincerely held faith served as the inspiration for most of his work, even though he wasn't a religious painter in the sense of Michelangelo or Caravaggio. Though not overt, there is a spiritual undercurrent that permeates all of his work. Van Gogh's faith discreetly influenced his artistic vision despite the classic paintings of flowers, fields, and self-portraits that he produced. He also pushed the bounds of art with his vivid color and rough brushwork. Although he was not well-liked when he was alive, his work, which is characterized by both the sublime and the chaotic, continues to enthrall viewers and inspire immersive displays that digitally augment his work with music and movement.

The admiration of van Gogh's artwork is deepened by knowing how he felt about religion. He was raised in a godly home and developed a love for sacred hymns as the son of a Dutch Reformed preacher. Steven Naifeh and Gregory White Smith, two of his biographers, contend that his enthusiasm for religion was a precursor to his artistic ability. His devotion indirectly inspired his art despite a poor attempt at theological studies and a misdirected desire to the pastorate. God had other plans for his gifting and abilities.

Ideological conflicts complicated Van Gogh's connection with his father and contributed to its complexity. He depicts a Bible and a novel by Émile Zola in "Still Life With Bible," which many see as a symbolizing their various worldviews. However, the Bible is shown as being strong and open, dwarfing the novel, suggesting that faith may have played a larger role in Van Gogh's life than first appears. His father's faith would have a lasting impact on Van Gogh.

Many works of art subtly convey this faith. Examples of clear biblical allusions include "The Raising of Lazarus," "The Good Samaritan," and "Pietà," while implicit allusions include "The Sower," which appears to mirror the parable from the Gospel, and pictures of gardens and olive trees like Gethsemane. Because of its connection to Easter, even an apparently straightforward painting of irises might conjure up the resurrection of Jesus.

Another piece of art with stars is "Café Terrace at Night," which may also include religious symbolism. It has been suggested by some that it might be Van Gogh's "Symbolist Last Supper," which depicts Jesus, the apostles, and Judas. Even if some people might find such readings improbable, they are supported by Van Gogh's own words: "Try to understand the last word of what the great artists, the serious masters, say in their masterpieces; there will be God in it."

Even though his work appears to be secular, Van Gogh's art reflects his religious beliefs. Our knowledge of his work is enhanced by this acknowledgment, which gives his masterpieces a richer context. We see how faith can influence and give meaning to creative expression via his paintings. Van Gogh's artistic legacy is a tribute to the interplay of faith, hardship, and beauty. We see how all beauty, when rightly directed, bears the imprint of the Divine Creator.

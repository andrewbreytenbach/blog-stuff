A Good Friday to Be a Calvinist


Everyday is a great day to be a Calvinist, but today is a great, not just good, Friday to be a Calvinist (did you catch my joke there?). Today, Christians all over the world remember what took place 2,000 years ago as the Son of God, Jesus Christ, was crucified on a criminal’s cross in order to reconcile God’s elect back to himself. Jesus paid their sin debt on their behalf, stood in their place, and satisfied the wrath of God for them. It is essential to the nature of this article to mention that Jesus did not die for the sins of the whole world. He did not die for Pontius Pilate’s sins or for Judas’ sins. No, Jesus died only for the sins of the elect, for those who would one day by God’s predestining grace trust in him and believe. That is what makes Good Friday great for the Calvinist. It is reveling in the sure and definite work of the death of Christ on our behalf. Good Friday is an occasion to celebrate the beautiful implications of definite atonement, also known as particular redemption, a doctrine that magnifies the love of God and the sufficiency of Christ's death on the cross.

Definite Atonement: A Glorious Display of God's Love

Definite atonement asserts that Christ's death on the cross was intended specifically for the elect – those chosen by God before the foundation of the world. Far from limiting the love of God, this doctrine reveals the depth and particularity of divine affection, demonstrating that God's love is not a vague, indiscriminate sentiment but rather a focused, intentional act.

By design, this doctrine highlights the particularity of God's love and the purposeful nature of the atonement. This particularity demonstrates God's unyielding commitment to those He has chosen to redeem. As Christ's death is not a mere potentiality, but a definitive act of love for the elect, it becomes a powerful testament to God's unwavering determination to save His people.

By securing the salvation of the elect through Christ's death, God's love is shown to be all the more glorious, as it culminates in the actual redemption of His chosen people. This understanding of God's love gives us greater cause for gratitude and praise as we contemplate the cross, knowing that our salvation was purposefully accomplished by the loving hand of our Heavenly Father.

The Sufficiency and Efficacy of Christ's Death

A common objection to definite atonement is the idea that it constrains the scope of Christ's sacrifice, suggesting that His death was insufficient to atone for the sins of all humanity. However, this misapprehension fails to grasp the true nature of the atonement's sufficiency and efficacy. It also falls apart on numerous other grounds, but this isn’t the blog post for that, so we’ll keep moving along.

Calvinists affirm the sufficiency of Christ's death to atone for the sins of all people. The distinction lies in the application of that atonement – it is applied specifically to the elect, ensuring its efficacy in securing their salvation. This understanding not only upholds the sufficiency of Christ's sacrifice but also emphasizes its effectiveness, revealing that the redemptive work of Christ on the cross is not a mere possibility but a guaranteed reality for the elect.

Here is the translation for us today: our salvation is not a possibility but a reality. For those in Christ, we know for certain that Jesus did in fact pay our sin debt and that we are reconciled back to God, declared righteous and covered in the blood of the lamb. Any doubts we have regarding this matter are shattered when we look at the blood-stained cross of Christ.

The Assurance and Joy of Salvation

Definite atonement offers believers an assurance of salvation that is grounded in God's unchanging love and the certainty of Christ's work on the cross. As the doctrine contends that Christ's death was intended specifically for the elect, it provides a solid foundation for the believer's confidence in their ultimate redemption. This assurance fosters a deep sense of joy and gratitude as believers rest in the knowledge that their salvation is secure, a precious gift bestowed upon them by a loving and sovereign God. It is a humbling reminder that our redemption is not dependent on our own merit or efforts, but solely on the grace of God and the finished work of Christ on the cross.

On a more personal note, it might be said like this: when Jesus hung on the cross, he didn’t have the whole world in mind. He was picturing your face. He was thinking of your name. Your sins flashed before his eyes as he hung up there and he did all of it for you. Not the whole world without exception, but specifically for you, Christian. And how amazing is that! Jesus Christ was bearing the full wrath of God on a cross and he was thinking of you. This is the glory of definite atonement.

Conclusion

On this Good Friday, as we remember Christ's sacrificial death, let us rejoice in the profound beauty of definite atonement. This doctrine illuminates the depth of God's love, the sufficiency and efficacy of Christ's sacrifice, and the assurance of salvation for the elect. We can embrace the fullness of these truths, finding in them a wellspring of gratitude and joy as we meditate on the cross and our Savior's love. May our hearts overflow with praise for the One who gave His life for us, accomplishing our eternal redemption and revealing the glory of God's grace.

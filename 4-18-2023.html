Unworthy Servant


"Hier stehe ich, ich kann nicht anders."


On this date in history, Martin Luther uttered those famous words at the Diet of Worms. They echo down the corridors of history as a powerful reminder of what one man, standing firm on the authority of the Word of God, is able to accomplish against a pagan opposition.

Leading Up to the Diet of Worms:

Martin Luther, that famous German monk and professor of theology, emerged as a key figure in the Protestant Reformation when he nailed his Ninety-five Theses to the door of All Saints' Church in Wittenberg on October 31, 1517. The Theses criticized the Roman Catholic Church's practices, particularly the selling of indulgences, which were documents that they claimed granted the forgiveness of sins. Luther's ideas quickly spread across Europe, leading to growing tensions between the Catholic Church and those who supported his views. Thus, the Reformation had begun.

In response to the challenge posed by Luther and his followers, Pope Leo X issued a papal bull (official decree) called "Exsurge Domine" on June 15, 1520. The bull condemned 41 of Luther's propositions as heretical and gave him 60 days to recant, or he would face excommunication. Luther, however, publicly burned the bull in December 1520, solidifying his defiance and further angering the Catholic Church. Gotta love Luther, right? The “Holy” Roman Emperor Charles V, who was also the King of Spain and had political interests in preserving the Catholic Church's authority, called for the Diet of Worms in 1521. The Diet was an imperial assembly of the Holy Roman Empire's estates, including high-ranking nobles and church officials, to address the growing religious conflict. Martin Luther was summoned to appear before the assembly to defend his ideas and then recant from his position.

The Diet of Worms (April 1521):

Luther arrived in the city of Worms, escorted by a contingent of supporters. The next day, he appeared before the Diet, presided over by Emperor Charles V. Johann Eck, a theologian and a representative of the Archbishop of Trier, presented Luther with a table covered in his writings and asked if he was the author and if he was prepared to recant his teachings. Luther acknowledged authorship but requested additional time to consider his response to the question of recantation. The assembly granted him a one-day reprieve, during which he prayed and wrestled with the Scriptures.

On April 18, 1521, Luther returned to the assembly. When asked again if he would recant, he responded in German:

"Unless I am convinced by the testimony of the Scriptures or by clear reason (for I do not trust either in the pope or in councils alone, since it is well known that they have often erred and contradicted themselves), I am bound by the Scriptures I have quoted and my conscience is captive to the Word of God. I cannot and will not recant anything since it is neither safe nor right to go against conscience. Here I stand; I can do no other. God help me. Amen."

Luther's famous words, "Here I stand; I can do no other. God help me. Amen," showcased his unwavering commitment to Scripture and his refusal to submit to the authority of the Catholic Church. They serve as a powerful reminder of what we ought to be committed to– the authority of the Word of God and not human wisdom or powers.

The Aftermath:

Following Luther's refusal to recant, the assembly was divided on how to proceed. Some advocated for harsh punishment, while others, particularly German princes, were more sympathetic to his cause. On April 25, 1521, Emperor Charles V declared Luther an outlaw in the Edict of Worms, banning his writings and placing a bounty on his head. However, Luther was able to escape capture with the help of his supporter, Frederick the Wise, Elector of Saxony, who arranged for Luther to be "kidnapped" and secretly transported to Wartburg Castle. While in hiding, Luther translated the New Testament into German, making the Bible more accessible to ordinary people. What man meant for evil, God meant for good.

The Diet of Worms marked a crucial turning point in the Protestant Reformation and solidified the divide between the Roman Catholic Church and the blooming Protestant movement. The event not only demonstrated Luther's unwavering commitment to his beliefs but also highlighted the growing support for his ideas among various segments of society as the Scriptures took root and the orthodox church grew.

The spread of Luther's ideas continued to gain momentum, and the Reformation took root in various European countries, leading to the formation of different Protestant denominations. In the long term, the Diet of Worms and the subsequent events of the Reformation contributed to the eventual decline of the Catholic Church's dominance in Europe and the rise of Reformed Churches. The emphasis on the individual's relationship with God and the importance of Scripture in Protestant theology would also lay the groundwork for the development of democratic ideals and the promotion of religious freedom, which we in America are benefactors of.

The Diet of Worms was a pivotal moment in the history of Christianity, as Martin Luther's refusal to recant his teachings marked a decisive split between the Roman Catholic Church and the emerging Protestant movement. The consequences of this event would reverberate throughout Europe and shape the course of Western history for centuries to come. Praise God for those mighty words– Hier stehe ich, ich kann nicht anders. Here I stand, I can do no other.

~ An Unworthy Servant

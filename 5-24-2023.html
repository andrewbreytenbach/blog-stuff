Unpacking the Covenants of Scripture


For today, I want to unpack at a very high level the concept of covenant theology. If you’ve never had the opportunity to do a deep dive into this, then the resource I would recommend with enthusiasm is “The Christ of the Covenants” by O. Palmer Robertson. You will not find a better resource out there to grasp what covenant theology is.

Fundamentally, reformed covenantal theology takes a comprehensive approach to understanding God's progressive revelation of himself to humanity throughout biblical history. Covenantal theology encourages a fuller understanding of God's self-disclosure through two fundamental covenants: the covenant of works and the covenant of grace. It is a key component of the Reformed tradition.

The Covenant of Works

The Garden of Eden is the birthplace of the covenant of works, often known as the covenant of life or nature. Adam served as the federal or covenantal leader of all people when God established this covenant (Rom. 5:12–14). According to Deuteronomy 30:15–20, this covenant required full obedience, making the justice and righteousness of God visible. The rules were simple: obeying brings life, disobeying brings death.

Adam and Eve broke God's command by eating the forbidden fruit, which caused them to lose their initial righteousness. Original sin is the belief that every member of the human race inherited Adam's wicked character and guilt as a result of the catastrophic event known as the Fall (Rom. 5:18–19).

The Covenant of Grace

The covenant of grace was created as a result of humankind's inability to uphold the covenant of works, offering a restorative strategy for fallen humanity. The grace covenant, revealed in Genesis 3:15, is gradually revealed via God's relationships with Noah, Abraham, Moses, and David, ultimately leading to Jesus Christ, the mediator of the New Covenant.

God made a special agreement with Abraham because it perfectly captures the spirit of the covenant of grace (Gen. 17:1–8). It confirms God's promises to be Abraham's God, to make him the patriarch of many nations, and to give his offspring the Canaanite land. This covenant, which was to be accepted via faith (Galatians 3:6–9), was founded on grace rather than human merit.

While reminding the Israelites of the covenant of works, the Mosaic covenant also clarifies the covenant of grace (Exod. 20:1–17). Moses' ceremonial and legal regulations made it clear that atonement was required and that God's people were expected to preserve a holy standard.

The Davidic covenant, which promised an everlasting reign and a King from David's bloodline, was a fulfillment of God's grace (2 Samuel 7:12–16).

Christ: The Fulfillment of the Covenants

A better covenant is being mediated by Jesus Christ (Heb. 8:6). He is the second Adam who, by perfectly obeying God's law, succeeds where the first Adam failed (1 Cor. 15:45). Christ's passive obedience in His death atones for sin while His active obedience grants righteousness to His people.

The grace covenant's promises are realized in Christ, ushering in the New Covenant. All other covenants are fulfilled and completed by this one, which guarantees eternal life to everyone who puts their faith in Christ.

Sacraments: Signs and Seals of the Covenant

The Lord's Supper and baptism within the New Covenant are two examples of sacraments that are visible marks and seals of God's covenant promises. Our admission into the covenant community is marked by baptism, which represents our identification with Christ's death and resurrection. On the other hand, the Lord's Supper serves as both a reminder of Christ's self-sacrificial death and a conduit of grace that feeds our faith.

Conclusion: Covenant of Redemption

The Covenant of Redemption (pactum salutis), a third covenant that is frequently emphasized in Reformed Theology, must also be mentioned. Despite not being specifically referenced in Scripture, it is implied by the salvation plan. This agreement was struck amongst the members of the Trinity in time immemorial. Redeeming action was prepared by the Father, carried out by the Son through obedience and sacrifice, and applied to the elect by the Holy Spirit (John 6:37–39; John 17).

The other two covenants are built upon this eternal covenant, which emphasizes the Trinitarian partnership in redemption. It reveals a God who purposely created a plan for the redemption of fallen mankind out of His unlimited kindness and mercy.

In conclusion, covenant theology constructs a broad story about God's eternal plan for salvation, with Christ serving as its climax. It contributes to our understanding of the Bible's coherence by showing how the Old and New Testaments are interwoven into a single narrative about God's merciful covenantal commitment to His people. The works-based covenant demonstrates the need for perfect righteousness for eternal life and fellowship with God. God's undeserved favor in promising and supplying this righteousness via Christ is illustrated through the covenant of grace. The covenant of redemption shows how the Godhead worked lovingly together to secure our salvation.

Covenant theology is essentially concerned with both the God of the Covenant and the Covenant of our God. From the initial promise of salvation in the Garden of Eden to its ultimate fulfillment in Christ, it is a theology of promise. We also get the benefits of God's covenant promises via trust in Jesus Christ, the promised seed and ideal covenant keeper. The believer lives coram Deo (before God's face), under the guidance of His covenant grace, and pursues a life of thanksgiving and obedience.

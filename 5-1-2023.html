A Rembrandt and the Christian Faith


It’s another Monday, and this calls for another biased analysis of a book that I have had the opportunity to read recently. This week I call your attention to a book centered on the intersection of art and faith. What role does art play in the life of the Christian? I just wrote a piece recently condemning the use of icons and images in worship, and it would not be out of the question for the cynical reader to retort “Does art even have any use in Christian faith then?” Well, I’m so glad you asked. I have just the book for you to read.

In "Rembrandt Is in the Wind: Learning to Love Art through the Eyes of Faith," author Russ Ramsey explores the intricate relationship between faith and art, specifically focusing on the works of the renowned painters throughout history– Michaelangelo, Rembrandt, Van Gogh, etc. Ramsey delves into the life and works of these artists, drawing parallels between the themes of their paintings and the teachings of Christianity. For example, what about the “David” is so magnificent that we should be drawn to appreciate it? The book also aims to engage readers with a broader understanding of the role of art in cultivating a deeper appreciation for truth, goodness, and beauty from a Christian perspective.

Ramsey's approach to discussing the intersection of faith and art is both scholarly and personal. By providing historical context and biblical precedent, he allows readers to gain insights into the theological necessity to value art as a believer. Yes, even though icons and images are a danger we face on the one hand, the complete neglect and disregard of art is just as dangerous. He also shares his own experiences and reflections, which adds depth and relatability to the narrative.

A key takeaway from the book is the emphasis on valuing truth, goodness, and beauty as essential aspects of Christian life. Ramsey demonstrates how art can be a powerful tool in conveying these values, as seen in various works. For example, Rembrandt's painting "The Return of the Prodigal Son" portrays the themes of forgiveness, redemption, and unconditional love, which are fundamental to Christianity. Van Gogh’s “Self-Portrait With a Bandaged Ear” is a deep picture of the human crisis of identity, a look at how we see ourselves when we are at our lowest and most vulnerable.

Moreover, the book encourages readers to appreciate the beauty in creation and the creativity of the Creator, the one in whose image we are made and the source of all art and creativity. Ramsey argues that by engaging with art, Christians can deepen their understanding of God's nature and his love for humanity. This artistic engagement, in turn, fosters spiritual growth and a stronger connection with God.

Reasons Christians should value truth, goodness, and beauty:

Reflection of God's nature: Truth, goodness, and beauty are inherent qualities of God, and by valuing these aspects, Christians can better understand and emulate His divine nature. Further, it isn’t a choice whether or not we want to emulate these qualities. Biblically, we are called to model these well and in the way God has modeled them.

Deepening faith: Engaging with art that embodies truth, goodness, and beauty can lead to a richer spiritual life and a closer relationship with God. It allows for a greater understanding of biblical themes, stories, and the emotions and perspectives of humans as they create the art.

Fostering empathy and compassion: Art often portrays the human experience, allowing Christians to better comprehend the struggles and joys of others. This understanding can inspire a greater sense of empathy and compassion for fellow human beings. Once again, if you know someone’s story and what they have experienced, you are in a betterer position to care for them and give them a presentation of the gospel. Yes, you know their temporal need, and now you can give them something even greater, something that will satisfy their eternal need.

Encouraging creativity: Valuing art and beauty can inspire Christians to explore their own creative talents, which can be a form of worship and a means of connecting with the Creator. Michelangelo was put on this earth to be a sculpture– there’s no doubt about it. When he was sculpting, he was glorifying God and walking in obedience. The same can be true of anyone else who chooses to be creative and engage in art.

Evangelism and cultural engagement: Art that reflects truth, goodness, and beauty can serve as a bridge for engaging with the broader culture, allowing Christians to share their faith in a compelling and relatable way. It can be a powerful way to give people a taste of the gospel message. Now, hear me clearly, a painting is not the gospel message per say. You need to still use words and tell them the good news of salvation and their sin condition that necessitates this. But art can serve as a means of starting a conversation or telling a story that relates in some way.

In conclusion, "Rembrandt Is in the Wind: Learning to Love Art through the Eyes of Faith" is a thought-provoking exploration of the intersection between faith and art. Through the lens of various artist's life and work, Russ Ramsey encourages Christians to appreciate and engage with art as a means to deepen their understanding of truth, goodness, and beauty, which ultimately enriches their spiritual lives. If you are as oblivious to the world of art as I am, I would encourage you to pick this one up and read it, learning all about how art glorifies the Sovereign King of the universe.

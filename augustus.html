Augustus: The First Emperor


”Now in those days a decree went out from Caesar Augustus, that a census be taken of all the inhabited earth. This was the first census taken while Quirinius was governor of Syria” (Luke 2:1-2). We find these words in the gospel of Luke, and they remind us of the historical veracity of the Bible. They are a testimony to the reliable and historical nature of the facts found in the Bible. Naturally, when we learn about these historical figures, it also might enlighten or help us understand better the biblical times in which the stories took place.

I recently finished “Augustus” by Adrian Goldsworthy. This behemoth biography was phenomenal, and I would recommend it with enthusiasm. The Roman Empire's foundations were laid by its first emperor, Augustus, whose reign marked a transformational period in Rome's history. In this blog post, I plan to summarize the life and legacy of this remarkable figure, drawing on the insights provided by Adrian Goldsworthy's comprehensive biography, which goes into various aspects of Augustus's rule, from his rise to power to his enduring influence on Rome and the world. That way, if you don’t have time to read, you get a good history lesson, but if your interest is peaked, then you'll order up a copy on Amazon and continue your study.

One more thing– consider this more of like a bunch of snapshot summaries of Augutus’ life. It isn’t in chronological order or thematic order. These are the notes I took as I made while I read the book, so if they seem like jumbled thoughts, it is likely because they are my jumbled thoughts.

A Formative Early Life

Born as Gaius Octavius, Augustus hailed from the Roman equestrian class and was the son of a successful military commander and politician. His life took a dramatic turn when his great-uncle, Julius Caesar (Et tu, Brute?), adopted him as his heir. This pivotal moment set the stage for Octavius's future ascent, as he continued to try and claim the legacy of his late father as he navigated the treacherous political landscape following Caesar's assassination, eventually forming the Second Triumvirate with Mark Antony and Lepidus.

The Struggle for Stability and Succession

Throughout his reign, Augustus grappled with the challenges of establishing a stable line of succession. Despite not having a biological son, he adopted multiple potential heirs, ultimately settling on his stepson Tiberius. This decision, coupled with his efforts to secure the imperial succession, had significant consequences for the stability of the empire.

Crafting a Benevolent Image

To legitimize his authority, Augustus skillfully used propaganda, art, and architecture. He commissioned monuments, temples, and public works projects celebrating his achievements and the Pax Romana, while also utilizing poets like Virgil and Horace to shape his image and the ideology of the empire. Though he himself tried his craft at poetry, no one seemed to think his poetry was anything special.

Navigating Complex Relationships

Augustus's relationships with his supporters, allies, and rivals were crucial to maintaining his position. He adeptly built a complex web of patronage, favors, and alliances, rewarding loyalty and punishing disloyalty. His shrewd political instincts and determination to maintain control over the empire were critical to his success.

The Darker Side of Augustus's Rule

Despite the image of a benevolent ruler, Augustus's reign was also marked by violence and strife. The proscription lists during the Second Triumvirate saw thousands of Roman citizens executed or their property confiscated, and rebellions, like the one led by Sextus Pompey, were brutally suppressed. When Augustus wanted something, it happened. His reign saw a new category for power in the ancient world. After becoming supreme ruler, Augustus was unchallenged. No one dared rise up against the Caesar.

Divine Authority and Personal Beliefs

Augustus's relationship with religion played a significant role in bolstering his rule. He capitalized on Julius Caesar's deification to elevate his own status as the son of a god, and the imperial cult began to develop around him. This religious connection influenced the Roman state's religious landscape and further legitimized his reign. In turn, this would make the persecution of Christians in a few decades much easier to support under the rule of the Roman empire, since Christians would not bow and worship these emperor ‘gods’.

Confronting Rivals and Challenges

Augustus faced numerous political, military, and external threats during his reign. He responded to uprisings, rebellions, and foreign conflicts, including the disastrous Battle of the Teutoburg Forest. His relationship with his chosen heir, Tiberius, also presented tensions as they navigated the complexities of power and succession.

Restoring Rome and Expanding the Empire

Augustus implemented constitutional and social reforms to stabilize the Roman state, improved infrastructure, and strengthened the economy. His military campaigns expanded the empire, while he maintained the loyalty of the legions through military reforms and a hierarchical reward system.

Leaving a Lasting Legacy

In addition to his many accomplishments as a statesman and military leader, Augustus's personal life and relationships were critical to his success. Despite facing numerous personal tragedies and setbacks, Augustus's unwavering devotion to his family and his inner circle of trusted advisers provided the support and stability he needed to maintain power and govern effectively.

Augustus's reign also set the stage for the future of the Roman Empire. His emphasis on a clear succession plan (to Tiberius) ensured a relatively smooth transfer of power to his successors, creating a lasting imperial system that would endure for centuries. The Roman Empire continued to expand and prosper under the Julio-Claudian dynasty and beyond, a testament to Augustus's vision and foresight.

Conclusion

Augustus stands as one of the most influential and consequential figures in world history. His ability to navigate the complexities of power and politics, as well as his innovative reforms and dedication to the well-being of the empire, ensured that Rome would emerge as a dominant force in the Mediterranean world. The Pax Augusta, the lasting peace and prosperity that characterized his reign, remains a shining example of his skill, vision, and impact as Rome's first emperor. As we reflect on the life and legacy of Augustus, it is clear that his remarkable achievements continue to resonate and inspire. Unfortunately for him, he spent his whole life opposed to God, and thus his eternity has been and will continue to be spent paying for those sins. How remarkable that God is able to use even pagan rulers to accomplish his purposes hereon earth. This is what we see in the opening chapters of Luke 2.

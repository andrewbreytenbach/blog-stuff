Beyond Beginning: The Eternal Self-Existence of God

It’s a Thursday, and that means it's time to dive deep into the fountain of theological knowledge. One of the most significant and foundational attributes of God is His aseity, which encompasses both His self-existence and self-sufficiency. This week, I aim to provide a comprehensive exploration of God's aseity, highlighting its importance in the theological understanding of the divine being and its implications for believers. I’ve modified the format slightly, so hopefully this will feel less like a sermon and more like a refreshing read in the morning with a cup of coffee.

The Concept of Aseity

Aseity is a term derived from the Latin words "a" (from) and "se" (itself), which together signify "from oneself." In our current theological context, the term aseity refers to the attribute of God's self-existence and self-sufficiency. The aseity of God asserts that He exists independently, without any external cause, and sustains Himself without the need for anything beyond His own being. Hence, this attribute belongs solely to God and to no one else.

Biblical Foundations for God's Aseity

The concept of God's aseity is deeply rooted in the biblical narrative and from our knowledge of who God is. Key passages from both the Old and New Testaments provide evidence of this divine attribute:

  • Creation Ex Nihilo (Genesis 1:1; Psalm 33:6, 9; Hebrews 11:3): In the account of creation, God is portrayed as creating the universe out of nothing (ex nihilo), demonstrating His ability to bring forth existence from non-existence. This act of creation underscores the self-existence of God, as He alone is the uncaused cause of all things.
  • God's Name (Exodus 3:14): When Moses asked God to reveal His name, God responded, "I AM WHO I AM." This name, often rendered as Yahweh or Jehovah in English, signifies the self-existent nature of God. He is not defined by anything external to Himself but rather is the very essence of existence.
  • Paul's Address to the Athenians (Acts 17:24-28): In his sermon at the Areopagus, Paul emphasized the self-sufficiency of God, declaring that He is not served by human hands as if He needed anything. Instead, God Himself gives life and breath to all things.
  • God's Independence (Romans 11:33-36): In his letter to the Romans, Paul extols the unsearchable wisdom and knowledge of God, asserting that all things come from Him, through Him, and to Him. This passage further emphasizes the self-sufficiency and independence of God.

Theological Implications of God's Aseity

The aseity of God carries several significant implications for our understanding of His nature and His relationship to creation:

  • God's Independence and Immutability: God's aseity implies that He is completely independent from creation and any external factors. As a result, God is immutable, or unchanging, because nothing can affect His essential nature or attributes.
  • God's Sovereignty and Providence: God's self-sufficiency ensures that He is sovereign over all creation. As the uncaused cause of all things, He holds the ultimate authority and power to direct and govern the universe according to His perfect will.
  • The Incomprehensibility of God: The aseity of God highlights the vast gulf between the Creator and His creation. Since God is self-existent and self-sufficient, He transcends human comprehension and understanding. This is not to say that we cannot know anything about God, but rather that our knowledge of Him is limited and derived from His self-revelation.
  • The Grace of God: The concept of God's aseity also sheds light on the grace with which He interacts with His creation. Since God is entirely self-sufficient, He is not obligated to provide for, sustain, or save His creation. Therefore, His acts of providence, sustenance, and salvation are all expressions of His unmerited favor and grace. This understanding underscores the gratuitous nature of God's love for humanity and His willingness to enter into a relationship with us despite our unworthiness.

The Importance of God's Aseity for Believers

For Christians, the attribute of aseity holds particular significance. It shapes how we understand our relationship with God and offers practical implications for our lives:

  • Humility and Dependence: Recognizing the aseity of God should lead to a posture of humility and dependence. As finite, created beings, we rely on God for our existence and well-being. Acknowledging our need for God fosters a spirit of gratitude and submission to His will. It also breaks the human instinct of thinking we are self-made, independent beings. We aren’t, but God is.
  • Assurance and Hope: The self-sufficiency of God offers believers a firm foundation for assurance and hope. Because God is unchanging and independent from creation, we can trust in His promises and His ability to accomplish His purposes. Our faith is rooted in a God who is not limited by external factors or the whims of creation.
  • Worship and Adoration: A proper understanding of God's aseity leads us to worship and adore Him for who He is. As the self-existent and self-sufficient Creator, God alone is worthy of our praise and devotion. His aseity inspires awe and wonder, as we contemplate the infinite nature of the One who is the source of all things.
  • Evangelism and Apologetics: God's aseity serves as an essential component of Christian evangelism and apologetics. When sharing our faith with others, we can point to the self-existence and self-sufficiency of God as evidence of His uniqueness among all other conceptions of deity. This attribute sets the God of the Bible apart from the gods of other religions and worldviews, reinforcing the exclusivity of the Christian faith.

Conclusion

The aseity of God is a crucial and foundational attribute that informs our understanding of our Sovereign Lord. It testifies to God's self-existence, self-sufficiency, and independence, highlighting the vast distinction between the Creator and His creation. As believers, the knowledge of God's aseity should inspire humility, dependence, worship, and a commitment to sharing the truth of the Christian faith with others. By contemplating the self-existent and self-sufficient nature of God, we are reminded of the profound mystery and majesty of the One in whom "we live and move and have our being" (Acts 17:28).

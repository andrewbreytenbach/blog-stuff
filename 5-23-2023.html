The Colossal Impact of Constantine the Great


May 22, 337, is a pivotal date in the history of Christianity since it was the day when Constantine the Great, a key actor in the Christianization of the Roman Empire, passed away in Nicomedia. An in-depth look at Constantine's life, conversion, and lasting impact paints a vivid picture of a man who, despite being a product of his era, would forever change the course of Christian history.

Constantine was the son of a powerful political family and was born in 272 AD. His mother, Helena, is regarded as a saint by the Orthodox, Oriental, and Roman Catholic faiths. His father, Constantius Chlorus, was a Roman magistrate who would eventually become emperor. Although Constantine's mother was largely responsible for his early exposure to Christianity, he spent a major portion of his early years in the typical Roman religious environment, worshiping the pantheon of established Roman gods.

The Battle of the Milvian Bridge, in which Constantine defeated his adversary, Maxentius, in 312 AD, marked the unmistakable turning point. Constantine credited his victory to a divine vision he had just before the fight, during which he saw a cross-shaped symbol in the sky along with the words "In this sign, conquer." His conversion to Christianity as a result of this life-altering event made him the first Roman emperor to do so.

The impact of Constantine's conversion on Christianity was enormous. Constantine introduced a number of measures that dramatically altered the standing of Christianity in Roman society in an empire where Christians were frequently ignored and persecuted. He issued the Edict of Milan in 313 AD, which effectively put a stop to the persecution of Christians and guaranteed religious tolerance for all faiths throughout the empire. Due to this edict, Christianity was able to flourish and go from being a persecuted minority religion to a broadly recognized religion. Postmillennialism, here we come!

The organization of the Christian Church started to take shape more clearly under Constantine's direction. He called the first ecumenical council, the Council of Nicaea, which tried to settle the Arian conflict (see last week's blog on the topic) and establish the Church's doctrines regarding the person of Jesus Christ, in 325 AD. At this meeting, the Nicene Creed, a foundational declaration of Christian belief, was written, providing a coherent theology for the developing Christian faith.

But Constantine left behind a complicated legacy. His support of Christianity not only helped to advance the religion but also prepared the way for the fusion of church and state, which would have long-lasting effects on human history. Constantine also maintained his worship of the sun god Sol Invictus alongside his conversion to Christianity, illustrating the complexity of religious change at this time and potentially even calling into question the validity of his conversion.

But there is no denying Constantine's influence on the development of Christianity. His actions altered the Roman Empire's topography and altered how the general populace viewed Christians and their social standing. There was an increase in Christian conversions as a result of the construction of churches, the granting of privileges to the clergy, and the freedom of the Christian faith to flourish. The one postmillennial who frequents this blog is starting to smile right around this point.

Furthermore, Constantine the Great's influence persisted after his passing. His sons, who took over as rulers, carried on his policy of favor toward Christians, assuring the steady expansion of Christianity throughout the Roman Empire. A bastion of Christianity developed under the Byzantine Empire, which saw Constantine as its founder.

In conclusion, Constantine the Great's life and legacy represent a crucial period in the development of Christianity. His conversion, support, and patronage of Christianity, symbolized by important events like the Council of Nicaea and the Edict of Milan, were crucial to the Christianization of the Roman Empire. His deeds altered not just the trajectory of the Roman Empire but also the face of world religion. Constantine's legacy continues to be a cornerstone for Christianity even as we commemorate the anniversary of his passing, a sign of the enduring influence of his authority. His example demonstrates the fundamental ways in which the Christian faith may change the course of history and the transforming power it possesses. Since he helped move Christianity from the periphery of society to its center, Constantine the Great is remembered by Christians today as more than just a Roman emperor.

A Biography on Bonhoeffer


I love to read. In particular, I love to read good books. It just so happens that writing about the good books (and bad ones) I’ve read incentivizes me to read more so I have more content to post on this fun little blog. I want to do that more. Today, I will turn my attention to a powerful biography I read recently that chronicles the life of a man who epitomized the courage to stand against tyranny: "Bonhoeffer: Pastor, Martyr, Prophet, Spy" by Eric Metaxas.

Metaxas' work is a comprehensive study of Dietrich Bonhoeffer, a German theologian, and his resistance against the Nazi regime. I’m going to summarize the major highlights of the book and offer three key takeaways for Christians today, as well as a word of caution. Let us turn first to the major highlights of the book.

Bonhoeffer's Early Life and Education

Eric Metaxas begins the book by taking us into Bonhoeffer's formative years, revealing how his affluent upbringing and family's intellectual background shaped his worldview. He details how Bonhoeffer pursued theology at an early age, earning doctorates from both Berlin University and Union Theological Seminary in New York City (before Union became the liberal madhouse it is today). It is during this time that Bonhoeffer began to develop his unique approach to faith, grappling with questions of ethics, social responsibility, and discipleship.

Bonhoeffer's Fight Against the Nazis

The heart of Metaxas' work lies in Bonhoeffer's unwavering opposition to the Nazi regime. From his initial criticisms of Hitler's rise to power to his active involvement in the Confessing Church movement, Bonhoeffer's life was marked by a commitment to justice and truth. This led him to take part in the underground seminary at Finkenwalde, providing spiritual guidance to pastors who refused to conform to the state-controlled German Church.

As the book progresses, Bonhoeffer's role as a double agent is revealed, as he works with the German military intelligence agency Abwehr to undermine the Nazi regime. His involvement in the plot to assassinate Hitler ultimately costs him his life, as he is executed just weeks before the end of World War II.

Bonhoeffer's Theology and Legacy

Throughout the biography, Metaxas weaves in Bonhoeffer's theological insights, providing readers with an understanding of the principles that guided his actions. From his concept of grace to his emphasis on the importance of community and discipleship, Bonhoeffer's ideas continue to influence Christian thought and practice today.

Now, I know that summary was very brief and very succinct, but the whole point is for you to go and read the book, so I can’t give you all the information. Let’s move on to a few takeaways for Christians.

Three Key Takeaways for Christians

The Cost of Discipleship

Bonhoeffer's life is a testament to the cost of true discipleship, as he sacrificed his own safety and ultimately his life to stand against evil. Christians today can draw inspiration from his courage and unwavering faith, recognizing that following Christ should require great sacrifices. The question each of us should ask is this: have we counted the cost? As Bonhoeffer has said, "Being a Christian is less about cautiously avoiding sin than about courageously and actively doing God's will." We are called to action in this Christian life, and this action is costly.

The Call to Social Responsibility

Bonhoeffer's theology emphasizes the importance of social responsibility (not social justice) and the need for Christians to be actively engaged in the world. He believed that faith must be accompanied by action, and that Christians have a duty to challenge injustice and promote peace. Bonhoeffer is famous for saying, “If I sit next to a madman as he drives a car into a group of innocent bystanders, I can't, as a Christian, simply wait for the catastrophe, then comfort the wounded and bury the dead. I must try to wrestle the steering wheel out of the hands of the driver.” This call to social responsibility is as relevant today as it was during Bonhoeffer's lifetime, even though it is much more nuanced and requires wisdom and discernment as we apply it.

The Power of Community

Bonhoeffer's emphasis on the role of community in the Christian life is another crucial takeaway from Metaxas' work. In an increasingly individualistic society, Christians must remember the importance of fellowship, mutual support, and accountability within the body of Christ. We were meant to do life together.

Conclusion

Eric Metaxas' biography of Dietrich Bonhoeffer, "Bonhoeffer: Pastor, Martyr, Prophet, Spy," is a powerful reminder of the impact one person can have when driven by faith and principle. Through his courage, theological insights, and unwavering commitment to justice, Bonhoeffer continues to inspire Christians today to hold to those same values. But here I must pause and offer a word of caution. Yes, everyone ought to buy this book and read it. Metaxas is a phenomenal author. But Bonhoeffer also had his flaws, and lest a reader of this blog think I wholeheartedly endorse him, let me just call it how it is. Yes, Bonhoeffer was very heavily influenced by Karl Barth, and this makes him neo-orthodox in many of his views. It has further implications, like the view of ultimate, universal truths and the doctrine of Scripture, but perhaps most alarming is what Bonhoeffer writes about salvation:

"In the body of Jesus Christ God is united with humanity, all of humanity is accepted by God, and the world is reconciled with God. In the body of Jesus Christ God took upon himself the sin of the whole world and bore it. There is no part of the world, be it never so forlorn and never so godless, which is not accepted by God and reconciled with God in Jesus Christ. Whoever looks on the body of Jesus Christ in faith can no longer speak of the world as if it were lost, as if it were separated from Christ."

That right there sounds an awful lot like universalism, doesn’t it? Orthodox Christianity would wholeheartedly reject this teaching, as would 2,000 years of church history, and the Bible itself. I say all this to remind us today that Bonhoeffer had his flaws, even though he was a good man who did much for the name of Christianity. So pick up the book, chew the meat, and spit out the bones.
